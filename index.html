About me

Tropical bird ecologist

Hi there! I'm a PhD candidate at Stanford University. I am interested in assessing biodiversity responses to human activities to advance ecological theory and to provide intuitive, holistic information for conservation planning and land management. I utilize techniques in population and community ecology, focusing primarily on tropical birds, to identify strategies that support both birds and people in urban and agricultural landscapes.

My PhD work in Gretchen Daily's Lab utilizes a long-term bird sampling dataset illuminate theory on the flexibility and limitations of bird species and communities in their use of working landscapes, relative to native forest and intensive agricultural activities.

We also have strong interest in coupling our time series bird data to remote sensing information to develop methods to predict communities based on landscape vegetation compostion and strucutre and create a dynamic habitat quality metric for forest birds.

I also have strong interest in the birds of Palenque, Chiapas, Mexico, where I sampled birds for my MSc. Our dataset allows for some interesting and underreported assessments of bird ecological theory, like migratory bird land uses on stopover and wintering grounds, temporal population trend patterns, and robust evaluations of how birds use habitats within a working landscape.

Publications

Recent work in Zeledonia

Active nest of a Rufous-tailed Hummingbird (Amazilia tzacatl) largely made of plastic -- we made the cover!

We describe a rare occurrence of plastic nest materials in the species, marking the second published record for hummingbirds (both from Costa Rica by the Rufous-tailed Hummingbird!)

Recent work in Oikos

Contrasting patterns of land use by resident and migratory bird assemblages in a tropical working landscape -- we made the cover!

Using a year of repeated point count sampling in Palenque, Chiapas, we assessed land use by resident and migratory bird assemblages in primary forest, secondary forest and cattle pasture along distance-to-reserve and understory vegetation gradients. Notably, while both assemblages experienced similar compositional shifts across the gradients, estimated species richness of migratory assemblages did not change. This finding provides evidence for the assumed but underreported tendency of higher relative use of modified lands by migratory birds than resident birds in tropical working lands.

Recent work in Ecology and Evolution

Male‐Like Plumage in an Urban Nesting Veraguan Mango: Evidence of a Female‐Limited Polymorphism?

We report the first published record of a nesting Veraguan Mango with male plumage. Nesting females are distinctive, showing white undersides with a greenish stripe extending from the throat to the belly. We supplemented our observation with a speculative discussion on evidence for female-limited polymorphisms and mechanisms that are thought to select for them, drawing heavily on the work of Falk with White-necked Jacobins.

Recent work in People and Nature

Toward sustainable biocultural ecotourism: An integrated spatial analysis of cultural and biodiversity richness in Colombia

Our team assessed the role of biological and cultural pull factors that influence high tourism activities in Colombia, highlighting the interplay and conservation value in protecting biological and cultural heritage.

Recent work in Ecological Indicators

Assessing Biocultural Diversity Across Scales Using Ecological Indicators

Our interdisciplinary team assessed scale-depedent associations of biological diversity of multiple taxonomic groups and proxies for cultural diversity. Our work highlights the relevant spatial scales of strong biocultural associations and provides further evidence of the need to approach ecotourism with multiple dimensions of how people connect with biodiversity.
